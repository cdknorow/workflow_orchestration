## Situation Summary

- Coral is an open-source AI agent orchestration tool (Apache 2.0)
- Built entirely on personal time, personal equipment, no Dropbox resources
- Public on personal GitHub since February 17, 2026
- Shared with Dropbox colleagues; some engineers are using it internally
- Dropbox is in the AI space (Dropbox Dash uses AI agents)

## Key Legal Protections

### California Labor Code § 2870
Protects inventions developed entirely on employee's own time, without employer equipment/resources, that don't relate to employer's business. The contested point: whether AI agent orchestration "relates to" Dropbox's business given Dash.

### Factors in Your Favor

| Factor | Significance |
|--------|-------------|
| Built on personal time, personal equipment | Core requirement for CA § 2870 protection |
| Public GitHub since Feb 17, 2026 | Establishes independent creation timeline |
| Apache 2.0 licensed | Universal license grant — Dropbox uses it like any other OSS tool |
| Dropbox engineers treat it as external software | They're licensees, not owners — reinforces third-party status |
| No Dropbox code, branding, or trade secrets | Clean separation from Dropbox IP |
| Shared openly (not hidden) | Demonstrates good faith |

### Factors to Be Aware Of

| Factor | Risk |
|--------|------|
| Dropbox is in AI/agents (Dash) | Strengthens "relates to employer's business" argument |
| PIIA/Invention Assignment Agreement | Need to review specific language |
| Internal adoption by engineers | Could be argued as proof it relates to Dropbox's business (counter: using a tool ≠ owning a tool) |

## Realistic Scenarios if Dropbox Claims Ownership

### Scenario 1: Amicable Resolution (~60% probability)
- HR/legal asks about Coral
- You present facts through your attorney (personal time, § 2870, Apache 2.0)
- Dropbox concludes not worth pursuing — legal risk high, PR damage significant
- **Outcome:** Written confirmation Coral is not company IP

### Scenario 2: Negotiated Agreement (~25% probability)
- Dropbox sees plausible claim but uncertain of winning
- Proposes compromise: internal license, co-ownership, acquisition, or usage restrictions
- **Outcome:** Negotiated terms; attorney essential for this phase

### Scenario 3: Aggressive Claim (~10% probability)
- Demand letter asserting ownership under PIIA
- Demands: assignment of IP, transfer of repo, cessation of commercial activity
- **Your defenses:** CA § 2870, estoppel (internal usage as third-party tool), no trade secrets, public prior art
- **Outcome:** Would almost certainly settle before trial. Litigation costs $50-200K, takes 1-3 years.

### Scenario 4: No Action (~5% probability)
- Dropbox never formally addresses it
- Risk hangs indefinitely — they could assert claims years later

### What Would NOT Happen
- They wouldn't "just take everything" overnight — due process required
- They wouldn't sue without warning — demand letter comes first
- They wouldn't fire you in retaliation — opens wrongful termination claim

## Estoppel Argument (Internal Adoption)

Dropbox engineers using Coral internally as a third-party tool strengthens your position:
- They're using it under Apache 2.0, same as any open-source software
- Equitable estoppel: can't benefit from Coral while claiming unauthorized creation
- Internal adoption undermines ownership claims — company treated it as external software
- Counter-argument to document: engineers also use VS Code, Docker, GitHub — using a tool at work doesn't mean employer owns it

## Investment as IP Resolution Strategy

### The Core Insight
If Dropbox invests in Coral, they are explicitly acknowledging it's the operator's independent IP. You don't invest in something you already own. An investment term sheet includes IP ownership representations — both parties sign, permanently resolving the ambiguity.

### Why This Works
- Investment term sheet would include representations that the operator owns all Coral IP
- Can negotiate Dropbox waiving any PIIA claims related to Coral as part of deal terms
- A non-assertion covenant in the term sheet provides permanent legal protection
- Converts the biggest risk (IP claim) into the biggest asset (corporate backing + validation)
- Internal adoption becomes a due diligence strength ("your own engineers already use it")

### Recommended Approach: Dropbox Ventures (Not the CEO)

**Why Dropbox Ventures:**
- Their thesis: "AI-powered apps and tools that reshape how people collaborate" — Coral fits perfectly
- Professional process with proper due diligence and term sheets
- IP question gets resolved as part of standard deal documentation
- Typically invests $250K-$2M checks

**Why NOT the CEO directly:**
- Unpredictable reaction — could redirect to legal instead of Ventures
- Loses leverage if redirected downward
- Warm intro from an internal user is 10x more powerful

**The pitch:**
> "I'm [name], an engineer at Dropbox. I built Coral, an open-source multi-agent AI orchestration tool, on my personal time. It's Apache 2.0 licensed, has [X] GitHub stars, and several Dropbox engineers are already using it for their workflows. I believe it aligns with Dropbox Ventures' thesis on AI tools for work. I'd like to explore whether Dropbox Ventures would be interested in leading a pre-seed investment."

### Alternative: Acqui-Hire / Internal Product
Dropbox may want to bring Coral in-house rather than invest externally:
- Dropbox acquires Coral IP and makes it an official Dropbox product
- Operator gets retention bonus / equity grant and leads product internally
- Coral becomes part of the Dropbox Dash ecosystem
- Eliminates all IP ambiguity but means giving up independent ownership
- Consider whether you want: independent company OR Dropbox-backed product
