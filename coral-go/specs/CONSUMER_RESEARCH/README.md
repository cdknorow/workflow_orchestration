# Consumer Research Study — Coral Positioning & Pricing

**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Method:** 3-round qualitative study (concept test, message testing, Van Westendorp pricing)
**Panel:** 10 participants, US-based, ages 21-70
**Limitation:** N=10, qualitative/directional only. Findings are patterns and hypotheses, not statistically significant conclusions.

## Round 1: Concept Test

### Method
Presented Coral's value prop in plain, jargon-free language. Asked participants to explain it back, rate interest/clarity/relevance (1-5), identify closest comparisons, and surface remaining questions.

### Key Findings

**Comprehension is excellent across the board.** Every participant — including non-technical ones — could accurately describe the product back. The "manager overseeing a team" analogy landed universally. Average clarity score: 4.2/5.

**Interest & Relevance Segmentation:**

| Segment | Interest | Relevance | Key Quote |
|---------|----------|-----------|-----------|
| In-market (Marcus) | 5 | 5 | "Exactly what I've been waiting for" |
| Adjacent (Priya, Aisha, Zoe, Jayden) | 3-4 | 2 | See value, want it for their domain |
| Out-of-market (Tom, Carlos, Sarah, Linda, Robert) | 1-2 | 1 | Understand it clearly, not their world |

**Top Recurring Themes:**

1. **"Why multiple instead of one good one?"** — Non-technical participants don't intuitively understand parallelization value. The target audience (Marcus) does immediately. Don't waste headline copy explaining this; use secondary copy or FAQs.

2. **"Who checks the AI's work?"** — Trust/quality assurance concern, especially from older participants. People want to know there's a human-in-the-loop.

3. **"Is this only for coding?"** — 4 participants independently asked about non-coding use cases (research, content, business). Signals latent demand beyond dev tools.

4. **"Is $49/yr the real price?"** — Low price creates suspicion in older/experienced buyers. They assume a catch.

5. **"How much babysitting?"** — Even interested buyers worry management overhead eats productivity gains.

6. **Merge conflicts / conflicting work** — Technical users immediately identify coordination as the hard problem.

**Mental Model:** People overwhelmingly map Coral to "project management for AI" (Asana, Monday.com, Jira — 7 mentions), not "developer tool." Secondary: "command center / control room" (NASA, military TOC — 3 mentions).

## Round 2: Message Testing

### Method
Tested 4 positioning statements in a 2x2 design (rational vs. emotional, individual vs. team). Rotated presentation order across two groups to control for primacy bias. Asked compelling score (1-5), gut reaction, "click to learn more" preference, and "share with someone" preference.

### Messages Tested

| | Individual Productivity | Team/Scale |
|---|---|---|
| **Rational** | **A:** "Parallelize your coding work. Run multiple AI agents on different tasks simultaneously — each in its own workspace — and ship in hours what used to take days." | **B:** "One dashboard to manage your AI agents. Assign tasks, prevent conflicts, and coordinate Claude, Codex, and Gemini — all working in parallel." |
| **Emotional** | **C:** "Stop feeding tasks to one AI and waiting. Coral lets you run a whole team of AI coders at once — so you can build at the speed you think." | **D:** "You already have the agents. Coral is what makes them work together." |

### Results

**Compelling Scores (avg):**
- **C: 3.9** (winner across every segment)
- B: 3.0
- A: 2.9
- D: 2.7

**"Click to Learn More" votes:** C: 7, B: 2, A: 1
**"Share with someone" votes:** C: 4, D: 3, B: 2, A: 2

**Message C is the decisive winner.** "Build at the speed you think" was called memorable by 8/10 participants.

### The Click/Share Split

Marcus (bullseye persona): *"C is what sells ME, but D is what I'd share because it makes me look like I found something cool before everyone else."*

This reveals a dual-messaging strategy:

| Placement | Message | Why |
|-----------|---------|-----|
| Landing page hero, paid ads | **C** | Acquisition — names the pain, promises the gain |
| Social media, developer communities | **D** | Virality — short, punchy, assumes sophistication |
| Features page, sales enablement | **B** | Credibility — Priya: "C gets me excited; B gets them to take it seriously" |
| ROI / business case collateral | **A** | Budget justification — Tom: "that's the ROI language that gets budget approval" |

### Additional Insights

- **"Parallelize" is dead for headlines** — 5/10 flagged it as jargon that would make them stop reading.
- **Message B belongs on the features page, not the hero** — multiple participants said it reads like a spec sheet.
- **"Shared message board" concept consistently confuses** — 4 participants weren't sure if they interact with it or if it's AI-to-AI. Save for deep product pages.
- **Tom's critique of D:** "Selling to yourself instead of the customer" — devastating for non-dev audiences, but that's fine because D targets insiders.

## Round 3: Pricing & Purchase Intent

### Method
Van Westendorp price sensitivity for in-market/adjacent segment. Simplified fair-price questions for out-of-market. Price reveal ($49/yr, normally $99/yr) presented AFTER open-ended questions to avoid anchoring.

### Van Westendorp Results (In-Market + Adjacent)

| Participant | Too Cheap | Good Deal | Getting Expensive | Too Expensive |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Marcus | <$20 | $60-80 | $120-150 | $250+ |
| Priya | <$20 | $60-80 | $150-200 | $300+ |
| Aisha | <$20 | $40-70 | $100-150 | $200+ |
| Zoe | <$20 | $40-60 | $100-120 | $200+ |
| Jayden | <$20 | $40-60 | $100-150 | $250+ |

**Derived Price Points:**
- Point of Marginal Cheapness: ~$20/yr
- **Optimal Price Point: ~$60-70/yr**
- Point of Marginal Expensiveness: ~$120/yr
- Acceptable range: $40-150/yr

### Out-of-Market Fair Price Estimates

All 5 out-of-market participants estimated fair price at **$100-200/yr** and flagged anything under $25-30 as suspicious. Every single participant said $49 feels too low for what's described.

### Purchase Intent at $49/yr (In-Market/Adjacent)

| Participant | Score (1-5) | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| Marcus | 4 | Would be 5 with free trial |
| Priya | 3 | Needs dev colleague validation |
| Aisha | 3 | Needs proof from collaborators |
| Zoe | 3 | Needs non-coding use case |
| Jayden | 3 | Needs non-coding use case |

### The $99-to-$49 "Launch Special" Verdict

**8/10 participants called it suspicious or saw through it.** Most common comparison: "Kohl's pricing" / "mattress store sale that never ends."

Key quotes:
- **Priya:** "I'd respond better to 'introductory pricing for early adopters' than 'normally $99, now $49.' The first signals exclusivity and reward for taking a chance; the second signals the product isn't worth $99."
- **Robert:** "If the company said 'we're pricing it low because we're new and want to earn your trust,' I'd respect that honesty."
- **Carlos:** "If it's good, people will pay. The fake discount thing feels like a mattress store sale that never ends."
- **Marcus:** "I mentally ignore the $99 and evaluate the $49 on its own merits."

**Generational split:** Jayden (21) admitted the discount "still works" on him despite seeing through it. Older participants (Robert, Carlos, Priya) rejected it entirely. The framing repels experienced buyers — exactly the wrong tradeoff for a professional tool.

### What Justifies $99/yr (Consensus)

1. **Proof of time savings** — case studies, before/after metrics, real data (8/10)
2. **Free trial** — every in-market participant wants to try before buying (10/10)
3. **Active development / not abandonware** — roadmap, regular updates (6/10)
4. **Community** — Discord with real users sharing workflows (5/10)
5. **Fast onboarding** — install-to-productive in 15 minutes (4/10)
6. **Transparency on renewal pricing** — no bait-and-switch (4/10)

## Recommendations

### Positioning

**Hero headline:** "Build at the speed you think"
**Hero subtext:** "Stop feeding tasks to one AI and waiting. Coral lets you run a whole team of AI coders at once — with clear roles, live visibility, and clean handoffs."

Use Message D ("You already have the agents. Coral is what makes them work together.") for social/community channels where the audience already understands AI agents.

### Pricing

**Raise to $69/yr.** This sits at the Van Westendorp optimal point, clears the "suspiciously cheap" threshold, and remains in Marcus's "just expense it" range.

**Kill the $99 strikethrough.** Replace with honest framing: "Early adopter pricing — $69/yr. Price increases as we add features." This signals honesty, rewards early users, and creates real urgency without fake discounts.

**Alternative:** Keep $49 but reframe as "Founding member pricing — $49/yr, locked in for life."

### Conversion Levers (Priority Order)

1. **14-day free trial** — universal requirement, highest-leverage conversion tactic
2. **Before/after demo** — concrete example of single-agent vs. Coral workflow
3. **Social proof** — real developer testimonials, Discord screenshots, community activity
4. **Transparent onboarding** — "install to productive in 15 minutes" promise
5. **Clear API cost separation** — make explicit that $69 is Coral only, users bring their own API keys

### Copy Kill List

- Drop "parallelize" from all headlines
- Drop "shared message board" from hero/feature headlines (save for deep pages)
- Drop strikethrough pricing ($99 anchor)
- Drop "launch special" language

### Future Signals (Not Actionable Now)

- 4/10 participants asked about non-coding use cases (research, content, business tasks). Monitor demand.
- Younger buyers (Jayden, Zoe) want TikTok-style demos and age-relevant social proof over enterprise case studies.
- Environmental footprint of multi-agent compute was raised by Zoe and Aisha — niche but vocal segment.
